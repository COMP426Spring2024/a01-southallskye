Persephone

Persephone is a pivotal figure in Greek mythology, embodying themes of growth, renewal, and the cyclical nature of life. She is best known as the queen of the Underworld and the goddess of spring growth, who presides over the rebirth of nature each year. Persephone is the daughter of Zeus, the king of the gods, and Demeter, the goddess of agriculture and fertility. Her story is central to the myth of the changing seasons. According to the myth, Persephone was kidnapped by Hades, the god of the Underworld, who desired her to be his wife and queen. Demeter, in her despair, neglected the earth, leading to a barren world where no crops could grow. Witnessing the suffering of humanity, Zeus ordered Hades to return Persephone to her mother. However, because Persephone had eaten pomegranate seeds in the Underworld — food of the dead — she was bound to return to Hades for a portion of each year. The myth explains the seasonal cycle: Persephone's annual descent to the Underworld marks the beginning of autumn and winter, periods when the earth lies barren and dormant, reflecting Demeter's grief. Her return to the surface world in spring symbolizes the bursting of life and the return of fertility to the earth.
